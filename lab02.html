AI Tool Evaluation

Introduction

The two tools I chose for my AI evaluation were Consensus and Claude. As I've begun taking more upper division courses, I have had a lot of research projects and papers, ranging from data centers run on renewable energy in Norway to evaluating the state of the economy and advising Pfizer whether or not to expand operations. Because of this, I was intrigued to explore Consensus, an AI powered research engine. I frequently use AI chat assistants for writing emails, checking code, creating outlines, and various other tasks. I've used ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, and DeepSeek, but I had never tried Claude before, which is why I chose it for my second AI tool to evaluate.

Tool 1: Consensus

Capabilities

Consensus AI is an AI search engine that searches through scientific research and academic papers to answer research questions. Consensus is very user friendly; it has a similar interface to ChatGPT or Copilot, which allows users to simply input their research question, and the tool does the rest, providing organized and easily understandable information. Citations are placed next to the information it is citing, and users can easily find and follow the link to further examine the document. Since outputs are presented through easily understandable consensuses to any research question, not all questions can be answered in one output, but the platform makes it very easy to ask follow up questions, which can then be answered by the tool. Answers rely on what is available in the research data and AI algorithms, so it is possible the platform can miss relevant research or misclassify results. This can lead to gaps or inaccuracies in its summaries and consensus metrics. The main function of Consensus is to present real, objective evidence, so the platform struggles to answer any questions that are inherently subjective or value based. The platform will present the evidence from available literature, but ultimately, it will not make a final subjective decision. For example, when the platform was asked to whether economic growth or environmental conservation should be prioritized, Consensus concluded that they should be pursued together.

Appropriate Use

Consensus AI would be a helpful tool for students that are starting a research project and need to find relevant sources to include in their research. While Consensus does provide summaries of the articles, it would be most beneficial for students to use the sources the tool pulls from their internet to start their research. Continued exploration of sources provided by the tool is crucial in ensuring full understanding of the text and its application in the context of the research. A poor use case of this tool would be to use the tool to search for medical, legal, or financial advice. While the tool can pull general findings from different sources, it cannot and should not apply them to a personal, actionable context.

Ethical Considerations

While Consensus AI is portrayed as an objective, helpful research tool, there are still ethical implications that must be acknowledged as responsible users of the platform. The name of the tool itself implies a contended and settled conclusion and simplified yes, or no outputs dismiss legitimate academic disagreement, minority viewpoints, and the provisional nature of science. Various biases are present in its inputs and algorithms, such as publication bias, database bias, and historical bias, which are further portrayed in its outputs. Tool 2: Claude

Capabilities

Claude is an AI chat assistant designed to assist with tasks such as writing, coding, and information retrieval. When testing its capabilities, the model transparently states the steps it took to produce the given output, helping users see where the information used to generate the response came from and allows further exploration of cited sources. Claude‚Äôs capabilities are especially good at document processing, complex reasoning, problem solving, and technical writing. When given a multi-page document with specified page numbers, the model was able to analyze the text and provide main themes, key insights, and analytical observations. Whenever presented with a complex logic puzzle, the tool was able to break down the question, carefully analyze each piece of information provided in the question, and come to a conclusion, accompanied by a step-by-step solution. However, drawback to the model is that the free version of Claude, which uses Sonnet 4.5, has a knowledge cutoff date of January 2025, so it must do a manual search for the information requested versus using the data it was trained with.

Appropriate Use

Claude could effectively be used as a research assistant to analyze multiple long documents and papers. With the ability to process up to 100,000 tokens, or approximately 75,000 words, in a single conversation, Claude is ideal for tasks that require deep contextual understanding of lengthy documents or books. However, Claude would not be useful as a real-time news source, as its knowledge cutoff is January 2025. Although the model can search the web for information to answer questions about events after the knowledge cutoff date, outputs may exclude relevant context, include misinformation and low-quality sources, and overall may not match the quality of output about events within the knowledge cutoff date.

Broader Reflections

I personally see myself using Consensus a lot in the future to assist with future research projects. The beginning of these research projects are always daunting, with uncertainty of where to start, what to search for, how to evaluate and analyze sources, and how to integrate them into the assignment. After exploring Consensus, I believe this would be a useful resource to begin gathering sources to start any research project. I would use Claude to help with analyzing long documents, but I wouldn't use the AI chat assistant exclusively due to the paywall limiting your number of inquiries. In the DCDA space, Consensus can provide the empirical foundation for research questions while Claude provides the analytical and generative engine to turn empirics into strategy, code, and communication. Bridging the gap between established knowledge and novel, data-driven action. What remains uniquely human is the shift from being the "doers" to being the makers of meaning, ethicists, and context-providers. As new AI tools emerge, it's important to ask questions such as "How is this AI tool trained?", "What are its known blind spots or biases", "Who isn't represented in this tool's training or design?", "How does it fail?", and "How will this tool need to evolve in 2 years?".
